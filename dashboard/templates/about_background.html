BACKGROUND

 

In recent years ARV ordering/reporting rates in Uganda have increased significantly, from c.50% to over 80%, and this has been a crucial step towards ensuring that ARVs are one day fully available at all of the country's accredited health facilities. Despite this important achievement, however, the Ministry of Health (MoH) has observed that although today there is more ARV order data being received by the country's central warehouses (via WAOS and paper submissions), the proportion of this information that is "correct" remains at low levels.

 

In short, there has been no parallel improvement in the quality of the order data being submitted.

 

This, of course, is a serious problem and one that continues to hinder health facility access to the ARVs that patients need.

 

To that end, a programme of work was brought underway in 2015 by MoH Pharmacy Division (PD) to begin addressing the issue, and a detailed process to identify problems and inconsistencies in order data has been initiated.

 

Key observed issues in the data include: inconsistency in submitting orders on time; significant lack of coherence in the numbers reported (e.g. between corresponding consumption and patient numbers, between NRTI volumes and NNRTI/PI volumes); volatility in reported numbers from one cycle to the next; incomplete order forms, especially with regards to patient numbers.

 

Such data quality issues continue to restrict good decision making in the ARV supply chain, thus causing stock outs and expiries at both the facility and warehouse level. Furthermore, these issues reflect fundamental shortcomings in areas such as: adherence to national ART guidelines; quality and correct use of base data sources; stock management; systematic checks within the ordering process; and HR and staffing levels within facilities.

 

Until now, largely Excel-based methods have been used to analyse ARV order quality, and resulting outputs have been leveraged in various ways, including: multiple presentations at national fora, production of facility reports for targeted support supervision visits and trainings on order quality for IPs and other partners.

 

Going forward, however, and in response to the need for long-term, sustainable, stakeholder-led improvement, this approach has been taken to the next step. The result is this initiative, the web-based ARV Order Quality Performance Tool, a system which will enable stakeholders to better understand and take ownership of improving ARV ordering. By providing data on performance it will equip IPs, District Health Teams, Warehouses and Central MoH to work with their facilities to identify root causes and, ultimately, resolve them.
